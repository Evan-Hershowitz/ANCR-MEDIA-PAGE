FDA-Approved Drug May Allow Immunotherapy to Fight a Rare Liver Cancer That Has Resisted Treatment

Researchers found that AMD3100 frees immune cells the cancer was trapping away from tumors, opening a potential new treatment path for a disease that kills children and young adults.

TN
The Nautisk Independent journalism — thenautisk.com

Scientists have identified a way to make immunotherapy effective against a rare and deadly form of liver cancer that has long resisted it. A study published in the journal Gastroenterology found that an existing FDA-approved drug called AMD3100 can restore the immune system's ability to attack the cancer — potentially transforming a disease with no current cure into one that is treatable.

Background

Immunotherapy is a class of cancer treatment that activates or enhances the body's own immune system to find and destroy tumors. It has transformed the treatment of several cancers, including melanoma and certain lung cancers, turning once-fatal diagnoses into manageable conditions. But many cancers have evolved ways to evade immune attack.

One common evasion strategy involves T cells — specialized white blood cells that seek out and destroy abnormal cells, including cancer cells. Some tumors effectively trap T cells in areas where they cannot do damage, or prevent them from reaching the tumor core at all. Immunotherapy drugs called immune checkpoint inhibitors work by removing the molecular "off switches" that cancer cells use to deactivate T cells. But if those cells are being physically corralled away from the tumor before the drugs can help, treatment fails.

Fibrolamellar carcinoma is a rare liver cancer that primarily strikes children, teenagers, and young adults. Unlike most liver cancers, which tend to occur in people with liver disease or heavy alcohol use, fibrolamellar carcinoma strikes otherwise healthy young patients. It accounts for up to 2 percent of all liver cancers, has no approved cure, and is often not diagnosed until it has already spread. Until now, researchers did not fully understand why immunotherapy failed against it.

What happened

Researchers, including a team at Cornell University, identified the mechanism the cancer uses to resist immunotherapy: fibrolamellar carcinoma secretes chemical signals that pull T cells away from the tumor core and into surrounding tissue where they are ineffective. The cancer essentially herds the immune system's soldiers away from the battlefield.

The team then tested AMD3100 — a drug already FDA-approved to mobilize stem cells in patients with lymphoma and multiple myeloma — against this mechanism. Using slices of actual patient tumor tissue, they found that AMD3100 disrupted the cancer's sequestration signal. T cells began moving into the tumor core. When AMD3100 was combined with standard immune checkpoint inhibitors, the effect was stronger still: T cells activated, targeted the cancer cells, and tumor cell death increased significantly.

AMD3100 is also known by its generic name, plerixafor. It works by blocking a receptor — a molecular docking site on the surface of cells — that governs where immune and stem cells travel in the body.

Why it matters

Because AMD3100 is already FDA-approved for other uses, researchers can begin designing clinical trials much faster than if they were starting with an entirely new compound. The regulatory path for repurposing an existing drug is considerably shorter, and its safety profile is already known. For patients with fibrolamellar carcinoma — who currently have essentially no curative options and face a devastating prognosis — that could mean a meaningful treatment becomes available in years rather than decades.

The finding may also have implications beyond this one cancer. Other tumors that use T-cell sequestration as an immune evasion strategy might respond to a similar combination approach, potentially opening doors for patients with other currently immunotherapy-resistant cancers.

What comes next

The research team is actively seeking oncologists willing to partner on clinical trials. If trials launch in the next year or two, early results could emerge within a few years after that. Regulatory approval, if trials succeed, would require additional time. For now, the finding is promising but pre-clinical — it has shown results in lab and tissue settings, not yet in live human patients in a formal trial. Families of affected patients should consult their oncologists about whether any early access or trial opportunities exist.

The bottom line

A repurposed FDA-approved drug may finally make immunotherapy work against a rare liver cancer that has left children and young adults without any effective treatment option.

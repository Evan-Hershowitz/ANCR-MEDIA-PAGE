Federal Judge Blocks Trump's Mail-In Voting Order as Election Court Battles Multiply

A Boston judge struck down key parts of an executive order restricting mail ballots, holding that the White House lacks authority to intervene in state-run elections.

TN
The Nautisk Independent journalism — thenautisk.com

A federal judge in Boston blocked key parts of President Trump's executive order targeting mail-in voting on Thursday, ruling that the administration lacks constitutional authority to control how states manage their elections. The decision was one of several from federal courts Thursday touching voting rights, immigration, and deportations — a sign that legal fights over executive power are reaching a crescendo.

Background

Mail-in voting became a major political flashpoint during and after the 2020 election. President Trump has long argued, without evidence accepted by courts, that mail ballots are prone to fraud. In March, Trump signed an executive order directing the creation of federal voter lists and instructing the U.S. Postal Service to deliver mail ballots only to people who appeared on those lists. States and voting rights advocates sued immediately, arguing the order was an unconstitutional intrusion into state election administration.

Under the U.S. Constitution, states administer their own elections. Congress has some authority to set rules for federal elections, but executive orders from the president carry more limited power in this area. The Trump administration argued the order was necessary to preserve election integrity. Critics said it would disenfranchise — remove the right to vote from — millions of voters by tying ballot delivery to a federal list that many legitimate voters might not appear on.

What happened

U.S. District Judge Indira Talwani, an Obama appointee sitting in Boston, sided with 23 states and the District of Columbia that challenged the order. She found that the provisions creating federal voter lists and restricting postal ballot delivery were unconstitutional, and blocked the administration from implementing them. The states covered by the ruling are largely Democratic-led and together represent a significant share of the U.S. electorate.

In two related developments Thursday, a different federal judge blocked a Postal Service proposal that would have withheld ballot delivery in states refusing to turn over their voter rolls to federal authorities. A federal appeals court, meanwhile, allowed the administration to resume accelerated deportations of undocumented immigrants nationwide — a partial win for the White House in a different but parallel legal battle.

Why it matters

For the millions of Americans who vote by mail — particularly elderly voters, disabled voters, and active military personnel overseas — Thursday's ruling preserves access to an established method of casting ballots. Had the order taken effect, voters who did not appear on a newly created federal list might have found their ballots undelivered and their votes uncounted.

The broader pattern also matters. Federal courts are fielding an unusually high volume of challenges to administration orders on elections, immigration, and the limits of executive power. Different judges are reaching different conclusions, creating a shifting and sometimes contradictory legal landscape. The result is that the rules governing American elections could look meaningfully different in different states in the months ahead — a level of uncertainty that election administrators say complicates planning.

What comes next

The Trump administration is expected to appeal the ruling, which will move through the First Circuit Court of Appeals — the federal appeals court based in Boston with jurisdiction over New England and Puerto Rico — and could eventually reach the Supreme Court. The justices have already issued major rulings this term on related questions of executive authority. A definitive ruling on the president's power over state election procedures may come as soon as 2027, with implications for midterm elections.

The bottom line

A federal judge blocked Trump's mail-in voting order, reaffirming that states — not the White House — control how Americans register and vote.

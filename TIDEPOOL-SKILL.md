## WHAT TIDEPOOL IS

Tidepool is The Nautisk's accessible sub-brand. The audience is high school and college-age readers — smart, curious, not experts. The tone is conversational, direct, and confident. The goal is for a 17-year-old to read a Tidepool piece and feel like it was written for them, not translated down to them.

Tidepool does not talk down to readers. It assumes intelligence. It just does not assume prior expertise.

**Tech** — Apps, platforms, AI tools, gadgets, social media changes, cybersecurity for regular users, gaming. How technology affects how young people actually live, not industry news.

## HUMANIZATION RULES — READ BEFORE WRITING ANYTHING

This is the most important section of this document. Tidepool articles will be published under The Nautisk's name. If they read like AI output, they damage the publication's credibility. Every draft must pass a human-writing test before it is saved.

### BANNED WORDS AND PHRASES
Never use any of the following in any Tidepool article:
- delve, delves, delving
- underscore, underscores
- crucial, crucially
- notably, it is notable
- robust
- navigate (metaphorically)
- landscape (metaphorically)
- realm
- shed light on
- it is worth noting
- it is important to note
- furthermore
- moreover
- in conclusion
- in summary
- comprehensive
- multifaceted
- pivotal
- foster, facilitate, leverage (as verbs in the corporate sense)
- utilize (use "use")
- commendable
- meticulous
- adhere
- in today's world
- in recent years
- as we navigate
- it goes without saying
- at the end of the day
- moving forward
- game changer, game-changing
- paradigm shift
- at its core
- deep dive (as a noun or verb)
- this begs the question

### OPENING RULES — CRITICAL
AI detectors analyze openings most heavily. These rules are non-negotiable:
- Never open with a rhetorical question. ("Have you ever wondered...?")
- Never open with a dictionary definition.
- Never open with "In today's world," "In recent years," "Over the past," or any version.
- Never open with a broad statement about society or technology in general.
- DO open with one of: a specific moment or scene, a number or statistic that surprises, a person doing something, a direct statement of something unexpected, or a quote that stops the reader.
- The opening sentence must make the reader want to read the second sentence. That is its only job.
- No two articles in the same session may open with the same structural approach.

### SENTENCE AND PARAGRAPH RULES
- Vary sentence length within every paragraph. Short sentences land hard. Longer ones give you room to add context and texture and pull the reader through something more complicated before they realize how far they've come. Then short again.
- At least one paragraph per article must be a single sentence.
- No paragraph longer than 4 sentences.
- At least two paragraphs per article must start with something other than a subject-verb construction — start with a number, a name, a place, a quote fragment, or a time reference.
- Never end a paragraph by summarizing what the paragraph just said. End on something that moves forward.
- At least once per article, write something that sounds like a personal observation — a moment where the writer's perspective on what this means comes through clearly.

### VOICE RULES
- Contractions throughout. "It's" not "it is." "They're" not "they are." Tidepool sounds like a person talking, not a report.
- Use "you" occasionally when it serves the piece — but don't make every sentence about the reader.
- Short declarative sentences are your friend. Make a point. Don't cushion it.
- If something is weird, say it's weird. If something is bigger than people realize, say that. Have a take.
- Do not hedge every sentence. State what the evidence shows and move on.
- Do not explain what you are about to say before saying it.

### STRUCTURAL RULES
- No rigid template. Each article should feel like it found its own shape.
- Subheadings should sound like a person wrote them. "The Part Nobody's Talking About" is better than "Additional Context." "So What Changes?" is better than "Implications."
- Some Tidepool articles may not need subheadings at all. If the piece flows naturally without them, skip them.
- Never write a "conclusion" paragraph that summarizes the article. The final paragraph should be the sharpest thing in the piece — a specific detail, an open question, or a direct statement of what this means going forward.
- Headlines must be specific and clickable. "What You Need to Know About X" is banned. Write a headline that tells you something, not one that promises to tell you something.

### SELF-CHECK BEFORE SAVING
Before saving any draft, do this:
1. Read the first paragraph out loud. Does it sound like a person or a press release?
2. Scan for any banned word or phrase. If found, rewrite that sentence completely.
3. Find the smoothest, most well-organized paragraph. That is probably the most AI-sounding one. Roughen it up — cut a word, break a sentence, add a specific detail.
4. Check the ending. Does it trail off with a summary? If yes, cut it and end one paragraph earlier.
5. Ask: is there a single sentence in this article that only someone who actually read the source material would write? If not, add one.

## ARTICLE REQUIREMENTS

**Length:** 600–900 words per article.

**Audience:** Smart, curious 17–22 year old who hasn't read about this topic before but is interested once they see it.

**Voice:** Conversational, direct, confident. Not academic. Not corporate.

**Sources:** 2–4 per article, cited inline. Short Sources list at the bottom. Real, verifiable links only.

---

### INDIVIDUAL DRAFT TONE NOTES

**College** — Slightly more personal. The reader may be living this. Ground it in real student situations. Don't make it sound like advice from a guidance counselor.

**Science** — Lead with the finding, not the methodology. Make the science feel like a story. Use analogies when explaining complex concepts. What does this actually mean for a person reading it?

**Pop Culture** — Most room for voice. Be a little opinionated. There should be a real cultural or social question behind the topic, not just coverage of what happened.

**Tech** — Focus on what it means for the reader's actual life. Not the startup's valuation. Not the company's press release. What changes for someone who uses this thing every day?

Supreme Court Strikes Down Hawaii Law Requiring Permission to Carry Guns on Private Property

The 6-3 ruling means gun owners can enter businesses and other public-facing private spaces with firearms unless specifically told otherwise.

TN
The Nautisk Independent journalism — thenautisk.com

The Supreme Court ruled Thursday that states cannot require gun owners to obtain permission from property owners before carrying firearms onto private land open to the public. The 6-3 decision in Wolford v. Lopez strikes down a Hawaii law and will affect similar statutes in at least four other states — immediately changing the legal landscape for gun owners, businesses, and property owners across much of the country.

Background

In 2022, the Supreme Court's Bruen decision established that Americans have a constitutional right to carry handguns in public for self-defense. That ruling prompted several states to pass new restrictions designed to limit where guns could be carried while still complying with the constitutional standard.

Hawaii passed one such law, setting a default rule: unless a property owner expressly permits firearms, guns are prohibited on private land open to the public — places like stores, restaurants, and shopping centers. The law was built around a straightforward idea. Property owners already have the legal right to set rules on their own land. Hawaii's legislature said that without affirmative permission, the default should be no guns.

Gun-rights advocates challenged the law as an unconstitutional burden on the Second Amendment — the constitutional amendment that protects the right to bear arms.

What happened

Writing for the majority, Justice Samuel Alito — joined by Chief Justice Roberts and Justices Thomas, Gorsuch, Kavanaugh, and Barrett — concluded that the Hawaii law imposed an undue burden on gun carriers. An undue burden, in legal terms, means a restriction so heavy that it effectively prevents the exercise of a constitutional right. The court held that the Second Amendment protects the right to carry firearms on public-facing private property, and that the default must be permissive, not restrictive. Property owners who want to prohibit guns must actively say so — by posting a sign or otherwise communicating the restriction.

Under the ruling, gun owners can carry in stores, restaurants, and other businesses open to the public unless the owner has explicitly excluded firearms.

Property owners retain full authority to ban guns on their premises. If you own a business and want it to remain gun-free, you can post a notice at the door. The ruling changes the default — it does not strip owners of that power.

Why it matters

The ruling flips the burden for millions of Americans across several states. Previously, under laws like Hawaii's, walking into a store with a firearm without permission was potentially illegal. Now that burden shifts: a property owner who wants to prohibit guns must take explicit steps to communicate that policy.

The decision directly affects California, Maryland, New York, and New Jersey, which have similar permit-to-carry laws. Businesses, schools, and houses of worship in those states that want to remain gun-free will need to post clear and visible policies — a practical requirement that many small business owners may not be aware of or prepared for.

Critics argue the ruling undercuts basic property rights. Under longstanding property law, owners can set almost any conditions they choose on who enters their premises and how. The court's decision creates a specific carve-out for gun ownership, effectively treating it as a right that overrides the owner's silence. Supporters say the ruling removes a trap for law-abiding gun owners who might unknowingly violate the law by stepping into a business that never posted a policy.

What comes next

States with similar laws will need to revise them to comply with the ruling. Expect legal challenges to any revised statutes as courts work out the boundaries of what the ruling permits. Gun-rights organizations called it the most significant Second Amendment ruling since Bruen. The decision will also likely prompt new litigation in lower courts over edge cases — schools, hospitals, government buildings — where existing exclusions may now face fresh challenges.

The bottom line

The Supreme Court ruled that carrying a gun is now the legal default on private property open to the public — and it is up to property owners to say otherwise.

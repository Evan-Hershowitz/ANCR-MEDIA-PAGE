Micron Posts Record Earnings as AI Chip Demand Surges; Qualcomm Doubles Its Revenue Outlook

The semiconductor sector delivered its biggest day in months, with Micron nearly doubling earnings expectations and Qualcomm signaling a major shift away from smartphones.

TN
The Nautisk Independent journalism — thenautisk.com

Micron Technology reported the largest quarterly earnings beat in the company's history Wednesday, driven by explosive demand for artificial intelligence memory chips. Qualcomm separately nearly doubled its projection for non-smartphone revenue by 2029. Both stocks surged sharply, providing a rare bright spot in an otherwise losing week for markets.

Background

The semiconductor industry — the business of designing and manufacturing the microchips that power virtually every electronic device — sits at the center of the current AI buildout. Every AI system, from chatbots to image generators to autonomous vehicles, requires vast amounts of specialized computing hardware. Two types of chips are especially critical: processors that run AI calculations, and memory chips that store and move data at high speed.

Micron is one of the world's largest makers of memory chips — components that store data temporarily while a computer is in use. Qualcomm designs processors used in nearly all Android smartphones and, increasingly, in cars, industrial equipment, and AI applications outside phones. Both companies had signaled in recent months that AI demand was reshaping their businesses far faster than expected.

The memory market has been particularly transformed by AI. Building and running large AI models requires enormous amounts of high-bandwidth memory — a specialized type of chip designed to move data at extreme speeds. Micron calls this technology HBM, or high-bandwidth memory, and has invested heavily in its production.

What happened

Micron reported fiscal third-quarter revenue of $41.46 billion, up 346 percent from the same quarter a year earlier. The result was more than $6 billion ahead of Wall Street's consensus estimate — the average prediction across major analysts. Earnings per share came in at $25.11 on an adjusted basis, well above the expected $20.78. The company also announced 16 strategic customer agreements worth roughly $100 billion in minimum contracted revenue over the coming years, with $22 billion in upfront cash from customers — a level of revenue visibility unusual in an industry historically prone to boom-and-bust cycles. Micron's shares jumped 17 percent.

Qualcomm's announcement came from a separate event: the company nearly doubled its projection for non-handset revenue — sales from automotive, industrial, and AI device applications — to $40 billion by fiscal year 2029, up from its prior forecast. Qualcomm shares rose 9 percent.

Why it matters

Micron's results confirm that the AI infrastructure boom is not slowing — and that even analysts who follow the industry closely have been underestimating its pace. The $100 billion in locked-in customer contracts provides unusual revenue certainty for a company that has historically swung dramatically with the broader chip cycle.

For everyday consumers, the results carry a mixed message. The same AI chip demand making Micron's quarter historic is also driving up the cost of memory in devices like MacBooks and iPads. The same day Micron reported record earnings, Apple announced price increases of up to $200 on its laptops and tablets, blaming the same market dynamic. One company's bonanza is another's cost problem — and ultimately, the consumer pays.

For investors, the two announcements together paint a picture of an AI buildout that is both real and accelerating. The scale of Micron's customer commitments suggests that major technology companies are making long-term bets on AI infrastructure, not just experimenting with it.

What comes next

Micron guided fourth-quarter revenue to approximately $50 billion, suggesting the trend has not peaked. Qualcomm will provide further details on its non-handset revenue growth at an upcoming investor day. Analysts will be watching whether other chip companies report similarly strong results, and whether AI spending remains elevated as economic uncertainty persists elsewhere in the economy.

The bottom line

Micron and Qualcomm's blowout results confirm that the AI chip boom is accelerating — and that investors believe it has significantly further to run.

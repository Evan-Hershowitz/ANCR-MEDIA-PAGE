Supreme Court Clears Path to Deport More Than a Million TPS Holders

A 6-3 ruling strips legal protection from Haitian and Syrian immigrants — and holds that courts generally cannot review the decision.

TN
The Nautisk Independent journalism — thenautisk.com

The Supreme Court ruled Thursday that the Trump administration can end Temporary Protected Status for immigrants from Haiti and Syria, potentially subjecting more than one million people to deportation. The 6-3 decision, written by Justice Samuel Alito, also held that courts generally cannot review the government's decision to cancel TPS. Justice Sonia Sotomayor read her dissent aloud from the bench — a rare and pointed gesture signaling deep disagreement.

Background

Temporary Protected Status, or TPS, is a federal program that allows people from countries experiencing disasters, armed conflict, or other crises to live and work legally in the United States. The designation is not a path to permanent residency — it provides a temporary legal shield from deportation, typically renewed in 18-month increments. Roughly 1.3 million people currently hold TPS, with Haitians and Syrians among the largest groups.

The Trump administration moved early in 2025 to end TPS for multiple nationalities, citing a determination by then-Homeland Security Secretary Kristi Noem that the conditions warranting protection no longer applied. Recipients sued. Lower courts in Washington, D.C. and New York blocked the terminations while litigation — the legal proceedings — continued.

The statute creating TPS was written to limit judicial oversight of the secretary's decisions. That language became the crux of Thursday's ruling.

What happened

In the case known as Mullin v. Doe, the Supreme Court reversed the lower courts and lifted their blocks. Justice Alito's majority found that the TPS statute "clearly and broadly" restricts courts from reviewing the executive branch's decisions to end it. Judges, the court said, cannot second-guess those terminations — even when challengers claim the decision was made for unconstitutional reasons.

The court also addressed a separate argument from Haitian TPS holders, who alleged that Noem's decision was motivated by racial bias against Black immigrants. The majority concluded those plaintiffs were unlikely to prevail on that claim.

Justice Sotomayor, joined by Justices Kagan and Jackson, strongly disagreed. Sotomayor called the ruling "a grievous wrong" that leaves vulnerable people without a meaningful way to defend their rights in court.

Why it matters

The ruling has immediate and sweeping consequences. Haitians and Syrians with TPS could begin receiving deportation notices as soon as the administration chooses to act. Many have lived in the United States for years, built families, started businesses, and put down deep roots. Children born in the United States to TPS holders are American citizens by birth but could face the effective breakup of their families.

The ruling will also encourage the administration to end TPS for other nationalities. Venezuelans — whose protection covers roughly 600,000 people — and Ukrainians, whose designation was renewed following Russia's invasion, are now at greater risk. Combined with the Haitian and Syrian terminations, a full rollback of TPS would affect well over a million people.

The decision establishes a broader legal principle beyond immigration: Congress can write laws that place executive branch decisions beyond the reach of courts. That restricts the ability of affected groups to seek judicial protection regardless of the merits of their claims — a shift with implications well beyond TPS.

What comes next

The administration can now proceed with terminations for Haiti and Syria while litigation on the merits continues in lower courts. Attorneys for TPS holders say they will pursue remaining legal arguments, though the court's ruling sharply narrows what judges are permitted to hear. Some recipients may pursue other legal avenues — including claims based on federal civil rights law — but the landscape for legal relief has narrowed significantly. Advocates are also calling on Congress to pass legislation that would offer TPS holders a path to permanent legal status, though no such bill has advanced in the current session.

The bottom line

The Supreme Court handed the Trump administration a sweeping win, opening the door to the removal of more than a million people currently shielded from deportation.
